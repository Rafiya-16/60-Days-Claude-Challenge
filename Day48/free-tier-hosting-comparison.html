How this was researched

Every score below is derived from documented facts, not vibes. Where the free-tier landscape changed materially in 2026 (Netlify's move to credit-based billing, Render's spin-down interval, Vercel's Hobby limits), the most recent official figures were used. Qualitative dimensions (uptime, deploy speed) are marked as editorial synthesis because none of these providers publish a free-tier SLA or a benchmarked deploy-time comparison.

Data point origins

  • Vercel — pulled directly from the official pricing table (vercel.com/pricing): 100 GB Fast Data Transfer, 1M edge requests, 1M function invocations, 4 Active CPU hours, all on Hobby. Hobby is explicitly restricted to non-commercial personal use per Vercel's Terms of Service.
  • Netlify — Netlify's official docs (docs.netlify.com) confirm the free plan moved to a 300-credits/month model on September 4, 2025. When credits run out, projects pause until the next billing cycle — confirmed on Netlify's own Free vs Personal and Free vs Pro comparison pages.
  • Render — Render's own blog post "Platforms with a real free tier for developers in 2026" (render.com/articles) states free web services spin down after 15 minutes of inactivity, cold start in roughly one minute, and the workspace gets 750 free instance hours/month. Free PostgreSQL databases expire 30 days after creation.
  • AWS Amplify — aws.amazon.com/amplify/pricing states the free plan is available for 12 months after account creation (or 6 months under a newer account-level Free Tier credit structure announced for accounts created after July 15, 2025 — see conflict note below), covering 1,000 build minutes, 5 GB storage, 15 GB data transfer.
Conflict resolved: Third-party aggregator sites disagreed on Netlify's exact bandwidth-to-credit conversion rate (10 vs 20 credits/GB were both cited by different blogs published weeks apart in 2026). Because Netlify's own documentation does not publish a fixed GB-to-credit rate on a single canonical page, this comparator treats Netlify's free-tier capacity as "credit-limited and variable" rather than asserting a specific GB figure, and flags it as an estimate in the table below.
Conflict resolved: AWS's own pricing page copy is ambiguous about whether the free plan window is 6 or 12 months for new accounts created after July 15, 2025 (it mentions both a "free for 12 months" hosting line and a 6-month free-plan-then-auto-convert policy in the same page). Both figures are shown in the sources panel rather than silently picking one.

What's an estimate, not a sourced fact

Uptime and "performance/CDN" scores are qualitative and synthesized from provider documentation about their delivery networks (all four run some form of global CDN) combined with the well-documented fact that Render's free web services literally go to sleep — a structural uptime disadvantage no other provider in this set has on its free tier. No provider publishes a numeric SLA for free-tier uptime, so these two scores are explicitly marked "estimate" in the data table rather than presented as hard benchmarks.
